The six stops

City by city, in ride order

Didi is China's Uber — it runs inside the Alipay app in English, no Chinese phone number needed, and it's the default for every station transfer below. Hong Kong is the exception: Octopus + MTR, taxis, or Uber. Each chapter's Station ↔ hotel block says when to ride rail and when to just take the car.

2Working from the road

Email — already solved

You'll have full email the entire trip. On the eSIM phone, everything works. On raw hotel wifi: M365/Outlook is NOT blocked in mainland China — just slow and occasionally flaky — while Gmail is hard-blocked (all of Google is). If wifi crawls, switch the phone to cellular and it's instant. Hong Kong has no firewall at all.

The laptop call — leave it home

The clean play: don't bring the work laptop. It's dead weight anyway — Dropbox has been blocked in China since 2014. Nothing on the itinerary needs a keyboard. The realistic workflow is triage from iPhone + iPad, flag things for return.

If the laptop comes anyway

Pause Dropbox syncing before departure (it can't sync there regardless; a paused client wakes up clean at home). Anything work-critical is reachable over the VPN or the phone's hotspot. FileVault on, strong password, never left unlocked in the room. Phones and iPads: Face ID + passcode is sufficient.

The Claude gotcha

Anthropic geo-blocks mainland China and Hong Kong — and many travel eSIMs exit through HK, so Claude can fail even on the eSIM while everything else works. Fix: VPN on the phone too, connected to a US / Japan / Singapore exit whenever you want Claude. Same story for ChatGPT. Applies in HK Oct 9–11 as well.

Hotel wifi scorecard

WORKS: Outlook / M365 / Teams (slow) · iMessage + FaceTime (the family channel) · Apple Maps, iCloud, App Store · Zoom (licensed China presence) · Trip.com, Alipay, WeChat, Didi
BLOCKED: Gmail + all Google · Dropbox · WhatsApp, Signal · Instagram, Facebook, X, YouTube · Wikipedia, Reddit, most Western news

Calls and the clock

Zoom works if a call is unavoidable — but China is 12 hours ahead of US Eastern: 9 AM at the office is 9 PM on the trip. Keep the US line active with roaming on, cellular data OFF — Alipay, banks, and airlines will text codes to it mid-trip. Enable wifi calling before you go.

3The kit — what goes in the bag

Screens

  • iPhone — the workhorse. Travel eSIM for data + US line for SMS only.
  • iPad — yes; the 5–6 hr train legs are what it's for. Cellular model: its own eSIM. Wifi-only: phone hotspot or hotel wifi + VPN.
  • Laptop — optional; the call is in §2. If it comes: VPN installed first, Dropbox paused.
  • Apple Watch — fine off the phone's wifi/Bluetooth; its cellular is dead there. Apple Pay works in HK, not the mainland.
  • Kindle / AirPods — fine. Load books and shows before flying — the Qinling tunnels kill signal for 45 minutes.

Power

  • Power bank — one, chosen carefully: ≤100 Wh (~27,000 mAh), capacity printed on the casing, name brand, carry-on only. China checks for CCC certification at security; unmarked banks get confiscated. It exists for the Oct 18 triple-train day.
  • Universal travel adapter — mainland is 220V with type A/C/I outlets (most accept US flat pins directly); Hong Kong is UK Type G. One universal adapter covers both.
  • No voltage converter needed — Apple chargers are dual-voltage. Anything with a heating element (hair dryer) won't work; the hotels have them.
  • One USB-C multiport GaN brick (65W+) charges phone, iPad, and Watch overnight from a single outlet — plus two cables per device; one lives in the daypack.
  • Every business-class train seat has an AC outlet + USB.

Leave at home

  • Garmin inReach / any satellite messenger — illegal in China without a license; confiscation risk at entry.
  • Drone — registration + license regime; not worth it.
  • Adderall / amphetamine-based meds — the border trap; see the tripwires below.

One bag that rolls

Packing — built around seven trains

Chinese high-speed rail has no checked luggage — everything you bring rides with you through seven stations, including two 15–20-minute transfer corridors on foot on the triple-train day. The rule that decides everything: if it doesn't roll at walking pace, it's too big.

The bags — four pieces

  • Main: a 21–22″ four-wheel spinner (carry-on size). The official G-train limit is 130 cm combined (~24″), loosely enforced — but the overhead racks fit carry-on size, and bigger goes to the end-of-car luggage towers (board a few minutes early to claim space). Check it free on the Cathay flights; you only ever carry it on rail days.
  • Daypack, 15–20L, zip-top — the everyday bag and train personal item: iPad, power bank, snacks, a layer, the umbrella. Zip-top moves fast through the X-ray at every station and metro entrance.
  • Packable duffel, folded flat in the spinner — flies home checked, full of Sam's tailoring, tea, and gifts.
  • Small crossbody/sling — the evening food-crawl rig: passport, phone, cash. Zipped, worn in front in station crowds.
  • Packing cubes — six hotels in 14 days; live out of the cubes and never really unpack.

Clothes — 8 days, one laundry stop

  • Pack ~8 days, not 14 — the Hilton Xi'an (3 nights, mid-trip) is the natural laundry stop.
  • Layers, not bulk: Guangzhou is still summer (72–86°F), Xi'an mornings hit 52°F. Short-sleeve base + one mid layer + one light jacket covers all six cities.
  • One pair of broken-in walking shoes — the wall, the quarters, the food crawls — plus slides for hotels and train legs.
  • Compact umbrella or light rain shell — Chengdu drizzles, Guilin showers, and HK/GZ carry a small typhoon-tail risk.
  • Dark, quick-dry fabrics — noodle broth and 90% humidity are both real.
  • One smart-casual outfit (hotel dinners, the opera).

The daily loadout (daypack, every morning)

  • Physical passport — required by law daily, and it literally is your train, museum, and hotel ticket. Zipped inner pocket; copies don't work at checkpoints.
  • Power bank + cable · phone loaded offline.
  • Pocket tissues + hand sanitizer — public restrooms rarely stock paper.
  • Compact umbrella · one layer.
  • Small-bill ¥ cash — and the ¥20 note for the Xingping photo on Oct 19.

Toiletries & pharmacy

  • Meds in original bottles, with a doctor's letter — the stimulant rule is the trip's #1 tripwire; only what the letter covers crosses the border.
  • Melatonin (the 12-hour flip) · Imodium + Pepto (street-food insurance) · ibuprofen · band-aids (430 steps) · sunscreen · bug spray for the Guilin river day.
  • Full-size liquids are fine on trains — only the flights care.
  • Skip the hair dryer — hotels have them, and yours wouldn't survive 220V anyway.

Documents & money

  • Passport + visa · paper photocopy in the spinner + photos in the camera roll.
  • The printed one-pager: bookings, confirmation numbers, card-issuer phone numbers (the Oct 1 checklist item).
  • 2+ no-foreign-fee cards, split — one in the daypack, one in the spinner.
  • ~US$200 emergency cash · ¥1,500–2,000 from a Bank of China ATM on arrival in Shanghai · small HK$ for the cash-only old guard (Australia Dairy Co., HK taxis).

Flight-day rules

  • ORD outbound: all five flights are ONE Cathay-stock ticket — so ask MIA check-in to through-tag the bag to HKG; on one ticket they should. If they refuse: claim at AA (T3), ATS train to T5, recheck with Cathay — the 5-hour layover keeps it relaxed either way.
  • The cabin-bag rule, both directions: passport, meds, electronics, and one change of clothes never leave your side — nothing you can't lose gets checked during a recheck.
  • Power bank: carry-on only, capacity printed on it (Tech §3).

What can actually sink this

The five real tripwires

Meds

Stimulant ADHD meds are effectively banned in mainland China. Sort prescriptions with your doctor months out — don't board with this unresolved.

Visa

Apply ~90 days out via COVA — nothing else matters if this slips. Embassy processing via courier runs 3–4 weeks door-to-door.

Real-name everything

Trains, Warriors, pandas are all tied to the passport number and booked weeks ahead. The calendar reminders are the system — don't freelance.

The Oct 18 triple-train day

Chengdu → Chongqing → Guiyang → Guilin all hinges on the first train. Confirm all three legs ticketed before leaving Xi'an.

The tea-ceremony scam

Anyone who approaches you in English near a tourist site and suggests going anywhere: smile, no, keep walking. Aimed exactly at solo American men.
